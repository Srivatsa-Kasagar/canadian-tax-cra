## Step 2 — Core tax concepts to apply

### Corporate side

**SBD-eligible income (first $500K active business income):**
- Federal rate: 9%
- Ontario combined: ~12.2%
- BC combined: ~11%
- Alberta combined: ~11%
- Quebec combined: ~12.2%

**Above SBD limit:**
- Federal rate: 15%
- Ontario combined: ~26.5%

Paying salary reduces corporate taxable income dollar-for-dollar. Paying dividends does not.

### Personal side — Salary

Use 2026 federal brackets:
- 15% on first $57,375
- 20.5% on $57,376–$114,750
- 26% on $114,751–$158,520
- 29% on $158,521–$220,000
- 33% above $220,000

Federal BPA: $16,129 (basic personal amount — 15% credit = $2,419 reduction)

Provincial rates by province:
- Ontario: 5.05% / 9.15% / 11.16% / 12.16% / 13.16% — BPA $11,865
- BC: 5.06% / 7.7% / 10.5% / 12.29% / 14.7% / 16.8% / 20.5% — BPA $11,981
- Alberta: 10% / 12% / 13% / 14% / 15% (thresholds: $148,269 / $177,922 / $237,230 / $355,845) — BPA $21,003
- Quebec: 14% / 19% / 24% / 25.75% — BPA $17,183

CPP on salary (2026):
- Employee + employer each pay 5.95% on earnings between $3,500 and $68,500
- Max combined CPP cost on salary = ($68,500 − $3,500) × 5.95% × 2 = $7,735/year
- Owner-operator pays both sides — total CPP cost up to $7,735 on first $65,000 salary
- CPP2 applies above $68,500 up to $73,200 at 4% (each side) — max $188/year combined

RRSP room generated by salary:
= 18% of earned income (salary), up to 2026 maximum of $32,490

### Personal side — Dividends

**Non-eligible dividends** (from SBD-taxed income — most small business):
- Gross-up: 15% (multiply dividend by 1.15 to get taxable amount)
- Federal dividend tax credit (DTC): 9.03% of grossed-up amount
- Provincial DTC varies — Ontario: 3.2876% of grossed-up amount

**Eligible dividends** (from income taxed at general rate above SBD):
- Gross-up: 38%
- Federal DTC: 20.73% of grossed-up amount
- Ontario DTC: 10% of grossed-up amount

No RRSP room from dividends.
No CPP cost on dividends.

## Step 3 — Run both scenarios

### Scenario A: 100% Salary

1. Corporate tax savings = salary amount × applicable corporate tax rate (salary deducted from corp income)
2. Employee + employer CPP = up to $7,735 on first $65,000 of salary
3. Personal federal + provincial income tax on salary (apply brackets, subtract BPA credits, subtract CPP employee premium credit)
4. RRSP room = 18% × salary (capped at $32,490)
5. Net personal after-tax = salary − personal taxes − employee CPP
6. Total tax cost = personal tax + employee CPP + employer CPP − corporate tax saved

### Scenario B: 100% Non-Eligible Dividends

1. No corporate tax deduction — corporation pays ~12.2% (ON) on the income first
2. No CPP cost (either side)
3. Personal tax on dividend: gross up by 15%, apply brackets, subtract federal DTC (9.03% × grossed-up) and provincial DTC
4. No RRSP room generated
5. Net personal after-tax = dividend − personal dividend tax
6. Total tax cost = corporate tax paid + personal dividend tax

### Scenario C: Optimal Mix (recommended)

The general optimal approach for most CCPC owners in Ontario at $100K–$200K income:

1. **Pay salary up to the CPP maximum** (~$68,500) to generate RRSP room and CPP benefits
2. **Pay remaining desired income as non-eligible dividends** — lower combined tax at many income levels
3. If corporate income exceeds SBD limit ($500K), eligible dividends may be more tax-efficient at higher personal income levels

Run calculations for the user's specific numbers to find the actual optimal split.

#### Key Tradeoffs

**Salary advantages:**
- Generates RRSP contribution room (18% of salary, up to $32,490)
- Builds CPP retirement benefits (if under 65 and CPP matters to you)
- Deductible to the corporation — reduces corporate tax
- Qualifies for disability insurance and other employment-linked benefits

**Dividend advantages:**
- No CPP cost — saves up to $7,735/year
- Simpler — no payroll account, no remittances, no T4
- At lower income levels, dividend tax credit often makes dividends more efficient
- No source deductions — better cash flow flexibility

#### RRSP Impact

Show the RRSP room generated under each scenario and note that unused RRSP room carries forward indefinitely — so if the user has substantial carry-forward room already, the RRSP benefit of salary is reduced.
