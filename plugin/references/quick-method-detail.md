# GST/HST Quick Method — Full Reference

## What Is the Quick Method?

The Quick Method is a simplified way to calculate your GST/HST remittance. Instead of tracking every input tax credit (ITC), you remit a flat percentage of your gross sales (including tax). You keep the difference between what you collect from customers and what you remit to CRA.

**Who can use it:** Businesses with taxable revenues of **$400,000 or less** in the previous fiscal year (including associated businesses), excluding zero-rated supplies.

**Who cannot use it:**
- Businesses with taxable revenues above $400,000
- Accountants, bookkeepers, financial consultants, actuaries
- Businesses that provide legal, dental, or medical services
- Municipalities, universities, schools, or hospitals (MUSH sector)
- Listed financial institutions
- Non-residents of Canada

---

## Remittance Rates (2026)

Rates are applied to **gross sales including the GST/HST charged** (i.e., the full amount your customer pays).

| Business Type | 5% GST Province | 13% HST (ON) | 15% HST Province |
|---|---|---|---|
| Service-based | 3.6% | 8.8% | 10.4% |
| Retail / wholesale | 1.8% | 5.0% | 6.0% |

**1% credit on first $30,000:** Under the Quick Method, you also receive a 1% credit on the first $30,000 of taxable supplies (including tax) in each fiscal year. This reduces your remittance further.

### Calculation Example (Ontario Service Business)

- Taxable sales in Q1: $80,000 (before HST)
- HST collected: $80,000 × 13% = $10,400
- Gross sales including tax: $90,400
- Quick Method remittance: $90,400 × 8.8% = $7,955.20
- 1% credit on first $30,000: $300 (if not yet used this year)
- **Net remittance: $7,655.20**

Under the standard method, if HST collected was $10,400 and ITCs were $2,800, net tax = $7,600. Compare both each year.

## Electing the Quick Method

**How to elect:**
- File Form GST74 (*Election and Revocation of an Election to Use the Quick Method of Accounting*) with CRA
- Can be done online through CRA My Business Account
- Alternatively, notify CRA in writing

**When the election takes effect:**
- Generally takes effect on the **first day of your fiscal year** in which you file the election
- Exception: New registrants can elect from the date of registration

**Key rule:** You must use the Quick Method for the **entire fiscal year** once elected — you cannot switch back mid-year.

---

## Revoking the Quick Method

- File Form GST74 to revoke
- Revocation takes effect on the **first day of your next fiscal year**
- After revoking, you must wait **at least one full fiscal year** before re-electing
- Exception: CRA may allow revocation mid-year in exceptional circumstances (e.g. business type changes)

---

## ITCs Under the Quick Method

When using the Quick Method, you **cannot claim most ITCs**. The only ITCs you may claim are:

1. GST/HST paid on **capital real property** (e.g. purchasing a commercial building)
2. GST/HST paid on **improvements** to capital real property

You cannot claim ITCs on operating expenses (rent, software, professional fees, etc.) — the Quick Method rate is designed to compensate for this.

---

## Annual Reporting Requirement

If you are an annual GST/HST filer using the Quick Method, you must:
- Report your Quick Method election on your annual return
- Provide total taxable supplies (including tax) and apply the correct remittance rate
- Track whether you have used the 1% credit on the first $30,000 of supplies

---

## When Quick Method Is Typically Advantageous

Quick Method tends to save money when:
- Your business has **low input costs** relative to revenue (high-margin services with few GST/HST-bearing expenses)
- You spend little on equipment, software, professional fees, or other ITC-eligible expenses
- Bookkeeping simplicity is a priority

Quick Method tends to be **less advantageous** when:
- Your business purchases a lot of supplies or equipment bearing GST/HST
- You have significant subcontractor costs
- ITCs under the standard method would exceed the Quick Method savings

**Recommendation:** Have your CPA run a comparison in the first year before electing.
